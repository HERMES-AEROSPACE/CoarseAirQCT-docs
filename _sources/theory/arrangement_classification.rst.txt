Arrangement Classification
==========================

After a trajectory converges (either :math:`t \geq t_{\max}` or the
products have separated beyond ``RpiMax``), CoarseAIR has to decide what
the collision produced: which atoms ended up bonded, in what
ro-vibrational state, and therefore which kinetic process this trajectory
represents. This chapter describes the classification algorithm and the
outcome categories it can assign.

All of the logic lives in
``src/Collision/MolecularState/MolecularState_Class.F90`` and
``src/Collision/Trajectories/TrajectoryPoint_Class.F90``.


Step 1 — Identify the final arrangement
---------------------------------------

The arrangement — which atoms are bonded to which — is found by sorting
the inter-atomic distances. A **heapsort** orders the pairs by
:math:`R_{ij}`; the smallest distance flags the most tightly-bound pair,
which forms the product diatom (for 3-atom systems) or pair of diatoms
(for 4-atom systems).

The arrangement is encoded as an integer **arrangement code**. For a
3-atom system ABC with pairs labelled :math:`\{1, 2, 3\}` (see
:doc:`qct`), the possible arrangements are:

.. list-table::
   :header-rows: 1
   :widths: 15 30 55

   * - Code
     - Bonded pair
     - Physical meaning
   * - 1
     - pair 1 (AB)
     - A–B bound, C free
   * - 2
     - pair 2 (AC)
     - A–C bound, B free
   * - 3
     - pair 3 (BC)
     - B–C bound, A free
   * - 0
     - none
     - full dissociation (A + B + C)

For a 4-atom system the code covers all four possible diatom-pair
partitions of {A, B, C, D} (AB/CD, AC/BD, AD/BC), plus partial- and full-
dissociation outcomes.


Step 2 — Classify each candidate diatom
---------------------------------------

Given the bonded pair, CoarseAIR classifies its internal state as
*bound*, *quasi-bound*, or *dissociated* using the effective-potential
picture. With reduced mass :math:`\mu` and final rotational quantum
number :math:`J'` (inferred from the total angular momentum — see below),
the effective radial potential is

.. math::

   V_{\text{eff}}(R)
      \;=\; V_{\text{diat}}(R)
         \;+\; V_{\text{c}}(R),
   \qquad
   V_{\text{c}}(R)
      \;=\; \frac{(J' + \tfrac{1}{2})^2}{2\mu R^2}

(the Langer correction :math:`J(J+1) \to (J+\tfrac{1}{2})^2` is used
throughout). The diatomic potential object locates the critical points:

- :math:`R_{\min}`, :math:`V_{\min}` — inner well minimum
  (``FindMinimum``).
- :math:`R_{\max}`, :math:`V_{\max}` — centrifugal barrier maximum
  (``FindMaximum``).
- :math:`V_\infty` — asymptotic potential.

The trajectory's internal energy in the candidate diatom is

.. math::

   E_{\text{int}} \;=\; E_{\text{int,K}} + V_{\text{diat}}(R_{\text{bond}}),

where :math:`E_{\text{int,K}}` is the kinetic energy in the diatom's CM
frame. CoarseAIR then applies the decision tree:

.. code-block:: text

   if R_bond > R_max:                       --> DISSOCIATED
   else:
      if E_int > V_max:                     --> DISSOCIATED (over the barrier)
      elif V_infty < E_int <= V_max:        --> QUASI-BOUND  (above the asymptote
                                                              but trapped behind
                                                              the centrifugal
                                                              barrier)
      else (E_int <= V_infty):              --> BOUND

The three outcomes are all physically meaningful:

- **Bound** — a genuine product diatom. Proceed to step 3.
- **Quasi-bound** — a metastable state that may eventually tunnel out
  of the centrifugal well. CoarseAIR counts it as a bound product but
  flags it in the output; post-processing can choose to treat it
  separately. This matters for near-dissociation levels, especially at
  low :math:`J`.
- **Dissociated** — no stable diatom; the trajectory contributes to a
  dissociation or full-atomisation channel.


Step 3 — Quantise the ro-vibrational state (if bound)
-----------------------------------------------------

For a bound (or quasi-bound) diatom, CoarseAIR assigns a final
vibrational quantum number :math:`v'` via the WKB action integral

.. math::

   v' + \tfrac{1}{2}
   \;=\; \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}
         \oint p(R)\, dR
   \;=\; \frac{1}{\pi\hbar} \int_{R_{\text{in}}}^{R_{\text{out}}}
         \sqrt{2\mu\,[E_{\text{int}} - V_{\text{eff}}(R)]}\, dR,

computed by ``DiatomicPotential%ActionIntegral``. The value is histogrammed
to the nearest integer (standard histogram binning, no Gaussian-binning
correction in the base code).

The rotational quantum number :math:`J'` comes from the total angular
momentum of the product diatom in its CM frame:

.. math::

   \|\mathbf{L}\|^2 \;=\; \left(J' + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2 \hbar^2.

Both quantities feed into the pre-computed level table to map the outcome
onto a specific state index (or bin index in coarse-grained mode — see
:doc:`sampling`).


Step 4 — Classify the process
-----------------------------

Given the initial and final state indices, the process type follows:

.. list-table::
   :header-rows: 1
   :widths: 22 78

   * - Process
     - Definition
   * - **Elastic**
     - Same initial and final pair, same :math:`(v, J)` up to binning
       tolerance.
   * - **Inelastic**
     - Same pair (no atom exchange), but different :math:`(v, J)`.
   * - **Exchange**
     - Different pair — the identity of the bonded atoms changed.
   * - **Dissociation**
     - No bound pair survives (3-atom: A + B + C; 4-atom: breakdown to
       atoms or atom + diatom subsets).
   * - **Exchange-dissociation**
     - 4-atom only: one of the two product diatoms has dissociated while
       the other reformed as an exchanged pair (e.g. AB + CD → AC + B + D).

The ``Processes_Type`` hierarchy under ``src/Processes/`` holds the
cross-section and rate-coefficient accumulator for each process category;
3-atom and 4-atom systems use distinct subclasses
(``Nb3Atoms_Processes_Class``, ``Nb4Atoms_Processes_Class``) because the
outcome space differs.


Indistinguishable-atom systems
------------------------------

When some atoms are chemically identical (e.g. H\ :sub:`3`, N\ :sub:`3`,
O\ :sub:`3`, H\ :sub:`4`, N\ :sub:`4`, O\ :sub:`4`), the arrangement code
alone is ambiguous: was this an *exchange* or just a re-labelling of the
same diatom? CoarseAIR handles this in two ways:

1. **Indistinguishable mode** — the cross-sections for exchange channels
   are summed together, matching the physical observable. This is the
   default for symmetric systems.

2. **Labelled mode** — atoms are artificially tagged (e.g. NaNbNcNd is
   the labelled counterpart of N\ :sub:`4`, and O18O18O16 labels ozone
   by isotope). The code then tracks each exchange pathway separately.
   Labelled runs are used to audit the indistinguishable-mode averaging
   and to compute isotope-specific rates.

Both modes use the same classification algorithm; the difference lives
in how the output is aggregated during post-processing.


Common pitfalls
---------------

- **Long-lived complexes.** On deep-well PES surfaces (C-containing
  systems, quasi-bound O\ :sub:`3` ensembles) a trajectory can spend
  tens of picoseconds in a four-atom complex before separating. If
  ``tmax`` is too short, the classification fires on the complex itself
  and labels the outcome as a mis-attributed "elastic" or
  "quasi-bound" event. Inspect the distribution of final times before
  trusting complex-rich systems.

- **Centrifugal barrier close to** :math:`E_{\text{int}}`. The
  quasi-bound / dissociated boundary is a thin one for near-dissociation
  levels; small numerical noise in :math:`E_{\text{int}}` can flip the
  classification. The ``CheckMaxAndMin`` and ``CheckTurningPoints``
  validators flag borderline cases.

- **Zero-point leakage.** Classical trajectories do not respect the
  :math:`v = 0` zero-point energy; low-energy vibrational populations
  are under-represented. This is a well-known QCT artefact; see the
  cross-references in :doc:`/reference/bibliography`.
