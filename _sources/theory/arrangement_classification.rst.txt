Arrangement Classification
==========================

After a trajectory converges (either :math:`t \geq t_{\max}` or the
products have separated beyond ``RpiMax``), CoarseAIR has to decide what
the collision produced: which atoms ended up bonded, in what
ro-vibrational state, and therefore which kinetic process this trajectory
represents. This chapter describes the classification algorithm and the
outcome categories it can assign.

All of the logic lives in
``src/Collision/MolecularState/MolecularState_Class.F90`` and
``src/Collision/Trajectories/TrajectoryPoint_Class.F90``.


Step 1 — Identify the final arrangement
---------------------------------------

The arrangement — which atoms are bonded to which — is found by sorting
the inter-atomic distances. A **heapsort** orders the pairs by
:math:`R_{ij}`; the smallest distance flags the most tightly-bound pair,
which forms the product diatom (for 3-atom systems) or pair of diatoms
(for 4-atom systems).

The arrangement is encoded as an integer **arrangement code**. For a
3-atom system ABC with pairs labelled :math:`\{1, 2, 3\}` (see
:doc:`qct`), the possible arrangements are:

.. list-table::
   :header-rows: 1
   :widths: 15 30 55

   * - Code
     - Bonded pair
     - Physical meaning
   * - 1
     - pair 1 (AB)
     - A–B bound, C free
   * - 2
     - pair 2 (AC)
     - A–C bound, B free
   * - 3
     - pair 3 (BC)
     - B–C bound, A free
   * - 0
     - none
     - full dissociation (A + B + C)

For a 4-atom system the code covers all four possible diatom-pair
partitions of {A, B, C, D} (AB/CD, AC/BD, AD/BC), plus partial- and full-
dissociation outcomes.


Step 2 — Classify each candidate diatom
---------------------------------------

Given the bonded pair, CoarseAIR classifies its internal state as
*bound*, *quasi-bound*, or *dissociated* using the effective-potential
picture. With reduced mass :math:`\mu` and final rotational quantum
number :math:`J'` (inferred from the total angular momentum — see below),
the effective radial potential is

.. math::

   V_{\text{eff}}(R)
      \;=\; V_{\text{diat}}(R)
         \;+\; V_{\text{c}}(R),
   \qquad
   V_{\text{c}}(R)
      \;=\; \frac{(J' + \tfrac{1}{2})^2}{2\mu R^2}

(the Langer correction :math:`J(J+1) \to (J+\tfrac{1}{2})^2` is used
throughout). The diatomic potential object locates the critical points:

- :math:`R_{\min}`, :math:`V_{\min}` — inner well minimum
  (``FindMinimum``).
- :math:`R_{\max}`, :math:`V_{\max}` — centrifugal barrier maximum
  (``FindMaximum``).
- :math:`V_\infty` — asymptotic potential.

The trajectory's internal energy in the candidate diatom is

.. math::

   E_{\text{int}} \;=\; E_{\text{int,K}} + V_{\text{diat}}(R_{\text{bond}}),

where :math:`E_{\text{int,K}}` is the kinetic energy in the diatom's CM
frame. CoarseAIR then applies the decision tree:

.. code-block:: text

   if R_bond > R_max:                       --> DISSOCIATED
   else:
      if E_int > V_max:                     --> DISSOCIATED (over the barrier)
      elif V_infty < E_int <= V_max:        --> QUASI-BOUND  (above the asymptote
                                                              but trapped behind
                                                              the centrifugal
                                                              barrier)
      else (E_int <= V_infty):              --> BOUND

The three outcomes are all physically meaningful:

- **Bound** — a genuine product diatom. Proceed to step 3.
- **Quasi-bound** — a metastable state that may eventually tunnel out
  of the centrifugal well. CoarseAIR counts it as a bound product but
  flags it in the output; post-processing can choose to treat it
  separately. This matters for near-dissociation levels, especially at
  low :math:`J`.
- **Dissociated** — no stable diatom; the trajectory contributes to a
  dissociation or full-atomisation channel.


Step 3 — Quantise the ro-vibrational state (if bound)
-----------------------------------------------------

For a bound (or quasi-bound) diatom the goal is to assign a final
ro-vibrational quantum-number pair :math:`(v', J')`, then match it
to a level index in the pre-computed level table. CoarseAIR uses a
semi-classical (WKB) procedure with six concrete sub-steps.

**Sub-step 3a — compute the product diatom's internal energy.**

In the product diatom's centre-of-mass frame, the internal energy is
the sum of the radial kinetic energy plus the potential:

.. math::

   E_\text{int}
   \;=\; \frac{p_R^{2}}{2\mu}
      \;+\; \frac{L^{2}}{2\mu R^{2}}
      \;+\; V_\text{diat}(R)
   \;=\; \frac{p_R^{2}}{2\mu}
      \;+\; V_\text{eff}(R; J'),

where :math:`\mu` is the pair's reduced mass,
:math:`R = \|\mathbf{R}_A - \mathbf{R}_B\|`, :math:`p_R` is the
radial momentum (the component of relative momentum along
:math:`\hat R`), and :math:`L^{2}` is the squared orbital angular
momentum. :math:`E_\text{int}` is a conserved quantity of the
product-diatom sub-Hamiltonian and is evaluated once at trajectory
termination from the Cartesian positions and velocities of the two
bonded atoms.

**Sub-step 3b — extract the rotational quantum number** :math:`J'`.

From the atomic velocities :math:`\dot{\mathbf{R}}_A`,
:math:`\dot{\mathbf{R}}_B` of the product pair in the CM frame, the
squared angular momentum is

.. math::

   L^{2}
   \;=\; \| \mu\,(\mathbf{R}_B - \mathbf{R}_A) \times
              (\dot{\mathbf{R}}_B - \dot{\mathbf{R}}_A) \|^{2}.

The Bohr–Sommerfeld quantisation with the Langer
(:math:`\ell(\ell + 1) \to (\ell + \tfrac{1}{2})^{2}`) correction
then gives

.. math::

   L^{2} \;=\; \hbar^{2}\,\left( J' + \tfrac{1}{2} \right)^{2},
   \qquad
   J' \;=\; \frac{\|\mathbf{L}\|}{\hbar} - \tfrac{1}{2}.

:math:`J'` is a positive real number here — the histogram binning
rounds it to the nearest non-negative integer, but the float value
is retained for diagnostic / Gaussian-binning use.

**Sub-step 3c — build the effective radial potential** with the
now-known :math:`J'`:

.. math::

   V_\text{eff}(R; J')
   \;=\; V_\text{diat}(R)
      \;+\; \frac{\hbar^{2}\left(J' + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{2}}{2\mu R^{2}}.

**Sub-step 3d — locate the radial turning points**
:math:`R_\text{in}`, :math:`R_\text{out}`. These are the two
roots of :math:`V_\text{eff}(R; J') = E_\text{int}`, bracketed by
:math:`R_\text{min}(J')` and :math:`R_\text{max}(J')` (the
effective-potential well minimum and centrifugal-barrier maximum
computed during level generation — see :doc:`level_generation`).
CoarseAIR solves each root by Newton–Raphson iteration with an
analytic derivative of :math:`V_\text{eff}`; convergence is
sub-percent in 3–5 iterations because the roots are smooth and
well-isolated.

**Sub-step 3e — evaluate the WKB action integral.** The Bohr–
Sommerfeld action is

.. math::

   S(E_\text{int}, J')
   \;=\; \oint p(R)\,dR
   \;=\; 2 \int_{R_\text{in}}^{R_\text{out}}
         \sqrt{2\mu\,\left[ E_\text{int} - V_\text{eff}(R; J') \right]}
         \,dR,

where :math:`p(R)` is the classical radial momentum

.. math::

   p(R) \;=\; \sqrt{2\mu\,\left[
               E_\text{int} - V_\text{eff}(R; J') \right]}.

The factor of 2 in front of the integral accounts for the two
half-orbits (inward and outward). The integrand has
**integrable inverse-square-root singularities** at both turning
points — :math:`p(R) \sim \sqrt{R - R_\text{in}}` near the inner
turning point and :math:`p(R) \sim \sqrt{R_\text{out} - R}` near the
outer — so the integral is finite despite the vanishing radicand.

CoarseAIR evaluates this action by *Gauss–Legendre quadrature* on
the normalised interval. With the affine substitution
:math:`R(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}(R_\text{out} + R_\text{in}) + \tfrac{1}{2}(R_\text{out} - R_\text{in})\,x`,
:math:`x \in [-1, 1]`, the integral becomes

.. math::

   S \;=\; (R_\text{out} - R_\text{in})
            \int_{-1}^{1}
            \sqrt{2\mu\,\left[ E_\text{int}
                              - V_\text{eff}(R(x); J') \right]}
            \,dx
       \;\approx\; (R_\text{out} - R_\text{in})
            \sum_{k = 1}^{N_q} w_k\,
            \sqrt{2\mu\,\left[ E_\text{int}
                              - V_\text{eff}(R(x_k); J') \right]},

where :math:`\{(x_k, w_k)\}_{k = 1}^{N_q}` are the Gauss–Legendre
nodes and weights of order :math:`N_q` (input line
``Nb of Quadrature Pts for Action Integral`` — default 50). The
nodes cluster slightly toward the endpoints, which mitigates the
:math:`\sqrt{}` singularity and yields spectral convergence on
smooth diatomics; for :math:`N_q = 50` the absolute error in
:math:`S` is well below :math:`10^{-6} \times 2\pi\hbar`, i.e.
:math:`\ll 1` vibrational level.

**Sub-step 3f — recover the vibrational quantum number** via the
Bohr–Sommerfeld rule:

.. math::

   v' + \tfrac{1}{2} \;=\; \frac{S(E_\text{int}, J')}{2\pi\hbar},
   \qquad
   v' \;=\; \frac{S}{2\pi\hbar} - \tfrac{1}{2}.

The result is a non-negative real number. CoarseAIR's default
``Final States Assigment Mthd = Histogram`` rounds to the nearest
integer and assigns the trajectory to the matching level index in
the pre-computed level table (produced in :doc:`level_generation`).
The alternative methods (Gaussian binning, QSS) retain the float
:math:`v'` in the output so that post-processing can apply a
different weighting scheme without rerunning the trajectories.

**Consistency check.** For a trajectory that starts from level
:math:`(v_i, J_i)` and does *not* experience a reactive
rearrangement, numerical energy conservation of the classical
dynamics guarantees :math:`S_\text{final} \approx S_\text{initial}`
up to integrator error. Inspecting the distribution of
:math:`v' \bmod 1` in a large ensemble is the standard diagnostic
for "zero-point leakage": a histogram uniformly spread across
:math:`[0, 1)` indicates trajectories slipping *below* their
zero-point energy and is the signature of the known QCT artefact
discussed in :doc:`/reference/bibliography` (Bonnet–Rayez, 1997).

**Code pointers.** The above procedure is implemented as a small
chain of elemental / pure routines on the diatomic-potential
object — see :doc:`pes_formats`:

- ``DiatomicPotential%TurningPoint`` → sub-step 3d
  (Newton–Raphson bracketing).
- ``DiatomicPotential%ActionIntegral`` (or
  ``ActionIntegralGamma`` for the complex-:math:`\Gamma`
  variant used for quasi-bound lifetimes) → sub-step 3e.
- ``DiatomicPotential%FindMinimum`` / ``FindMaximum`` →
  :math:`R_\text{min}(J')` / :math:`R_\text{max}(J')` bracket
  for the turning-point search.

Each of these is called in the hot path of
``MolecularState%FindState`` (``src/Collision/MolecularState/``),
so making them fast is important; they are all ``pure elemental``
and auto-vectorise over the batch of concurrently-finalising
trajectories.


Step 4 — Classify the process
-----------------------------

Given the initial and final state indices, the process type follows:

.. list-table::
   :header-rows: 1
   :widths: 22 78

   * - Process
     - Definition
   * - **Elastic**
     - Same initial and final pair, same :math:`(v, J)` up to binning
       tolerance.
   * - **Inelastic**
     - Same pair (no atom exchange), but different :math:`(v, J)`.
   * - **Exchange**
     - Different pair — the identity of the bonded atoms changed.
   * - **Dissociation**
     - No bound pair survives (3-atom: A + B + C; 4-atom: breakdown to
       atoms or atom + diatom subsets).
   * - **Exchange-dissociation**
     - 4-atom only: one of the two product diatoms has dissociated while
       the other reformed as an exchanged pair (e.g. AB + CD → AC + B + D).

The ``Processes_Type`` hierarchy under ``src/Processes/`` holds the
cross-section and rate-coefficient accumulator for each process category;
3-atom and 4-atom systems use distinct subclasses
(``Nb3Atoms_Processes_Class``, ``Nb4Atoms_Processes_Class``) because the
outcome space differs.


Indistinguishable-atom systems
------------------------------

When some atoms are chemically identical (e.g. H\ :sub:`3`, N\ :sub:`3`,
O\ :sub:`3`, H\ :sub:`4`, N\ :sub:`4`, O\ :sub:`4`), the arrangement code
alone is ambiguous: was this an *exchange* or just a re-labelling of the
same diatom? CoarseAIR handles this in two ways:

1. **Indistinguishable mode** — the cross-sections for exchange channels
   are summed together, matching the physical observable. This is the
   default for symmetric systems.

2. **Labelled mode** — atoms are artificially tagged (e.g. NaNbNcNd is
   the labelled counterpart of N\ :sub:`4`, and O18O18O16 labels ozone
   by isotope). The code then tracks each exchange pathway separately.
   Labelled runs are used to audit the indistinguishable-mode averaging
   and to compute isotope-specific rates.

Both modes use the same classification algorithm; the difference lives
in how the output is aggregated during post-processing.


Common pitfalls
---------------

- **Long-lived complexes.** On deep-well PES surfaces (C-containing
  systems, quasi-bound O\ :sub:`3` ensembles) a trajectory can spend
  tens of picoseconds in a four-atom complex before separating. If
  ``tmax`` is too short, the classification fires on the complex itself
  and labels the outcome as a mis-attributed "elastic" or
  "quasi-bound" event. Inspect the distribution of final times before
  trusting complex-rich systems.

- **Centrifugal barrier close to** :math:`E_{\text{int}}`. The
  quasi-bound / dissociated boundary is a thin one for near-dissociation
  levels; small numerical noise in :math:`E_{\text{int}}` can flip the
  classification. The ``CheckMaxAndMin`` and ``CheckTurningPoints``
  validators flag borderline cases.

- **Zero-point leakage.** Classical trajectories do not respect the
  :math:`v = 0` zero-point energy; low-energy vibrational populations
  are under-represented. This is a well-known QCT artefact; see the
  cross-references in :doc:`/reference/bibliography`.


Derivations and further detail
------------------------------

**Why Langer's** :math:`(J + \tfrac{1}{2})^{2}`. Direct quantisation
of the 3-D central-field Hamiltonian gives the centrifugal term
:math:`\hbar^{2} \ell(\ell + 1)/(2\mu R^{2})` with :math:`\ell`
integer. The semi-classical (WKB) treatment introduces a near-origin
singularity that Langer (1937) showed is corrected by replacing
:math:`\ell(\ell + 1) \to (\ell + \tfrac{1}{2})^{2}`. This makes the
WKB turning-point analysis at :math:`R \to 0` quantitatively correct
without touching the long-range behaviour. CoarseAIR uses the Langer
correction consistently throughout level generation, final-state
quantisation, and the centrifugal-barrier classifier.

**Effective-potential derivation.** Separating the 3-D radial
Schrödinger equation into radial and angular parts, the radial
equation for :math:`u(R) = R\,\psi(R)` reads

.. math::

   -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2\mu}\frac{d^{2} u}{dR^{2}}
   \;+\; \left[ V_{\text{diat}}(R)
              \;+\; \frac{\hbar^{2}\,(J + \tfrac{1}{2})^{2}}{2\mu R^{2}}
         \right] u
   \;=\; E\,u,

which is one-dimensional with the effective potential
:math:`V_{\text{eff}}(R; J) = V_{\text{diat}}(R) + V_{\text{c}}(R; J)`
defined in the main text above. For sufficiently large :math:`J`,
the centrifugal barrier :math:`V_{\text{c}}(R; J)` adds a maximum
at some :math:`R_{\max}(J) > R_{\min}(J)`; states with
:math:`V_\infty < E < V_{\max}(J)` are classically trapped behind
this barrier — the quasi-bound regime.

**Heapsort for arrangement classification.** Finding the tightest
pair among :math:`\binom{N}{2}` inter-atomic distances is an
:math:`O(N \log N)` operation by heapsort, implemented in
``src/Tools/heapsort.F90``. For 3-atom systems
(:math:`\binom{3}{2} = 3` distances) the sort is trivial; for
4-atom systems (:math:`\binom{4}{2} = 6` distances) it is still
cheap. The same routine is used whenever the code needs to order
quantities by value (e.g. sorting levels by internal energy during
preprocessing).

**WKB action integral — quantitative.** At a bound eigenvalue
:math:`E_{vJ}` the Bohr–Sommerfeld condition

.. math::

   \oint p(R)\,dR
   \;=\; 2 \int_{R_\text{in}}^{R_\text{out}}
         \sqrt{2\mu\,[E_{vJ} - V_{\text{eff}}(R; J)]}\,dR
   \;=\; 2\pi\hbar\,\left(v + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)

agrees with the exact radial Schrödinger eigenvalues to leading
order in :math:`\hbar`. The correction terms scale as
:math:`\hbar^{2}` and are typically <1 % of the level spacing for
heavy-atom diatoms — negligible for CoarseAIR's purposes. The
integral is evaluated by Gauss–Legendre quadrature (``Nb of
Quadrature Pts for Action Integral`` in ``CoarseAIR.inp``, default
50 points). For the level generator's use see :doc:`level_generation`;
for the final-state quantisation see the main text above.

**Gaussian-binning alternative.** The histogram binning CoarseAIR
uses rounds the WKB action to the nearest integer. Bonnet and
Rayez (1997) proposed *Gaussian binning*: weight each trajectory's
contribution to the final level :math:`v'` by a Gaussian of width
:math:`\sigma` centred on the actual :math:`v`-value, with
:math:`\sigma \sim 0.1` typically. This penalises trajectories
whose final action lies far from any quantised level — a proxy
for suppressing ZPE-violating outcomes. Not yet implemented in
the base CoarseAIR; tracked on :doc:`/developer/roadmap`.

**What the classifier does for 4-atom systems.** The 3-atom decision
tree generalises by partitioning the four atoms into the best
diatom pair first (by smallest two of the six inter-atomic
distances, subject to consistency: the two selected pairs must
share no atom), then applying the effective-potential classifier
to each candidate diatom independently. The 4-atom outcome space
therefore includes: two bound diatoms (inelastic or exchange,
depending on atom identities), one bound diatom + two free atoms
(single dissociation), four free atoms (full atomisation), and
various quasi-bound / predissociating mixes. The encoding in
``arr_f = 16 m + n`` captures one diatom at a time; 4-atom
trajectory output carries :math:`(J_k, v_k, J_l, v_l,
\text{arr}_f)` and the post-processor aggregates these into
process channels (see :doc:`/user/input_reference` for the
process-index encoding).
