Arrangement Classification

After a trajectory converges (either \(t \geq t_{\max}\) or the products have separated beyond RpiMax), CoarseAIR has to decide what the collision produced: which atoms ended up bonded, in what ro-vibrational state, and therefore which kinetic process this trajectory represents. This chapter describes the classification algorithm and the outcome categories it can assign.

All of the logic lives in src/Collision/MolecularState/MolecularState_Class.F90 and src/Collision/Trajectories/TrajectoryPoint_Class.F90.

Step 1 — Identify the final arrangement

The arrangement — which atoms are bonded to which — is found by sorting the inter-atomic distances. A heapsort orders the pairs by \(R_{ij}\); the smallest distance flags the most tightly-bound pair, which forms the product diatom (for 3-atom systems) or pair of diatoms (for 4-atom systems).

The arrangement is encoded as an integer arrangement code. For a 3-atom system ABC with pairs labelled \(\{1, 2, 3\}\) (see Quasi-Classical Trajectories), the possible arrangements are:

Code

Bonded pair

Physical meaning

1

pair 1 (AB)

A–B bound, C free

2

pair 2 (AC)

A–C bound, B free

3

pair 3 (BC)

B–C bound, A free

0

none

full dissociation (A + B + C)

For a 4-atom system the code covers all four possible diatom-pair partitions of {A, B, C, D} (AB/CD, AC/BD, AD/BC), plus partial- and full- dissociation outcomes.

Step 2 — Classify each candidate diatom

Given the bonded pair, CoarseAIR classifies its internal state as bound, quasi-bound, or dissociated using the effective-potential picture. With reduced mass \(\mu\) and final rotational quantum number \(J'\) (inferred from the total angular momentum — see below), the effective radial potential is

\[V_{\text{eff}}(R) \;=\; V_{\text{diat}}(R) \;+\; V_{\text{c}}(R), \qquad V_{\text{c}}(R) \;=\; \frac{(J' + \tfrac{1}{2})^2}{2\mu R^2}\]

(the Langer correction \(J(J+1) \to (J+\tfrac{1}{2})^2\) is used throughout). The diatomic potential object locates the critical points:

  • \(R_{\min}\), \(V_{\min}\) — inner well minimum (FindMinimum).

  • \(R_{\max}\), \(V_{\max}\) — centrifugal barrier maximum (FindMaximum).

  • \(V_\infty\) — asymptotic potential.

The trajectory’s internal energy in the candidate diatom is

\[E_{\text{int}} \;=\; E_{\text{int,K}} + V_{\text{diat}}(R_{\text{bond}}),\]

where \(E_{\text{int,K}}\) is the kinetic energy in the diatom’s CM frame. CoarseAIR then applies the decision tree:

if R_bond > R_max:                       --> DISSOCIATED
else:
   if E_int > V_max:                     --> DISSOCIATED (over the barrier)
   elif V_infty < E_int <= V_max:        --> QUASI-BOUND  (above the asymptote
                                                           but trapped behind
                                                           the centrifugal
                                                           barrier)
   else (E_int <= V_infty):              --> BOUND

The three outcomes are all physically meaningful:

  • Bound — a genuine product diatom. Proceed to step 3.

  • Quasi-bound — a metastable state that may eventually tunnel out of the centrifugal well. CoarseAIR counts it as a bound product but flags it in the output; post-processing can choose to treat it separately. This matters for near-dissociation levels, especially at low \(J\).

  • Dissociated — no stable diatom; the trajectory contributes to a dissociation or full-atomisation channel.

Step 3 — Quantise the ro-vibrational state (if bound)

For a bound (or quasi-bound) diatom the goal is to assign a final ro-vibrational quantum-number pair \((v', J')\), then match it to a level index in the pre-computed level table. CoarseAIR uses a semi-classical (WKB) procedure with six concrete sub-steps.

Sub-step 3a — compute the product diatom’s internal energy.

In the product diatom’s centre-of-mass frame, the internal energy is the sum of the radial kinetic energy plus the potential:

\[E_\text{int} \;=\; \frac{p_R^{2}}{2\mu} \;+\; \frac{L^{2}}{2\mu R^{2}} \;+\; V_\text{diat}(R) \;=\; \frac{p_R^{2}}{2\mu} \;+\; V_\text{eff}(R; J'),\]

where \(\mu\) is the pair’s reduced mass, \(R = \|\mathbf{R}_A - \mathbf{R}_B\|\), \(p_R\) is the radial momentum (the component of relative momentum along \(\hat R\)), and \(L^{2}\) is the squared orbital angular momentum. \(E_\text{int}\) is a conserved quantity of the product-diatom sub-Hamiltonian and is evaluated once at trajectory termination from the Cartesian positions and velocities of the two bonded atoms.

Sub-step 3b — extract the rotational quantum number \(J'\).

From the atomic velocities \(\dot{\mathbf{R}}_A\), \(\dot{\mathbf{R}}_B\) of the product pair in the CM frame, the squared angular momentum is

\[L^{2} \;=\; \| \mu\,(\mathbf{R}_B - \mathbf{R}_A) \times (\dot{\mathbf{R}}_B - \dot{\mathbf{R}}_A) \|^{2}.\]

The Bohr–Sommerfeld quantisation with the Langer (\(\ell(\ell + 1) \to (\ell + \tfrac{1}{2})^{2}\)) correction then gives

\[L^{2} \;=\; \hbar^{2}\,\left( J' + \tfrac{1}{2} \right)^{2}, \qquad J' \;=\; \frac{\|\mathbf{L}\|}{\hbar} - \tfrac{1}{2}.\]

\(J'\) is a positive real number here — the histogram binning rounds it to the nearest non-negative integer, but the float value is retained for diagnostic / Gaussian-binning use.

Sub-step 3c — build the effective radial potential with the now-known \(J'\):

\[V_\text{eff}(R; J') \;=\; V_\text{diat}(R) \;+\; \frac{\hbar^{2}\left(J' + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{2}}{2\mu R^{2}}.\]

Sub-step 3d — locate the radial turning points \(R_\text{in}\), \(R_\text{out}\). These are the two roots of \(V_\text{eff}(R; J') = E_\text{int}\), bracketed by \(R_\text{min}(J')\) and \(R_\text{max}(J')\) (the effective-potential well minimum and centrifugal-barrier maximum computed during level generation — see Level Generation (Numerov method)). CoarseAIR solves each root by Newton–Raphson iteration with an analytic derivative of \(V_\text{eff}\); convergence is sub-percent in 3–5 iterations because the roots are smooth and well-isolated.

Sub-step 3e — evaluate the WKB action integral. The Bohr– Sommerfeld action is

\[S(E_\text{int}, J') \;=\; \oint p(R)\,dR \;=\; 2 \int_{R_\text{in}}^{R_\text{out}} \sqrt{2\mu\,\left[ E_\text{int} - V_\text{eff}(R; J') \right]} \,dR,\]

where \(p(R)\) is the classical radial momentum

\[p(R) \;=\; \sqrt{2\mu\,\left[ E_\text{int} - V_\text{eff}(R; J') \right]}.\]

The factor of 2 in front of the integral accounts for the two half-orbits (inward and outward). The integrand has integrable inverse-square-root singularities at both turning points — \(p(R) \sim \sqrt{R - R_\text{in}}\) near the inner turning point and \(p(R) \sim \sqrt{R_\text{out} - R}\) near the outer — so the integral is finite despite the vanishing radicand.

CoarseAIR evaluates this action by Gauss–Legendre quadrature on the normalised interval. With the affine substitution \(R(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}(R_\text{out} + R_\text{in}) + \tfrac{1}{2}(R_\text{out} - R_\text{in})\,x\), \(x \in [-1, 1]\), the integral becomes

\[S \;=\; (R_\text{out} - R_\text{in}) \int_{-1}^{1} \sqrt{2\mu\,\left[ E_\text{int} - V_\text{eff}(R(x); J') \right]} \,dx \;\approx\; (R_\text{out} - R_\text{in}) \sum_{k = 1}^{N_q} w_k\, \sqrt{2\mu\,\left[ E_\text{int} - V_\text{eff}(R(x_k); J') \right]},\]

where \(\{(x_k, w_k)\}_{k = 1}^{N_q}\) are the Gauss–Legendre nodes and weights of order \(N_q\) (input line Nb of Quadrature Pts for Action Integral — default 50). The nodes cluster slightly toward the endpoints, which mitigates the \(\sqrt{}\) singularity and yields spectral convergence on smooth diatomics; for \(N_q = 50\) the absolute error in \(S\) is well below \(10^{-6} \times 2\pi\hbar\), i.e. \(\ll 1\) vibrational level.

Sub-step 3f — recover the vibrational quantum number via the Bohr–Sommerfeld rule:

\[v' + \tfrac{1}{2} \;=\; \frac{S(E_\text{int}, J')}{2\pi\hbar}, \qquad v' \;=\; \frac{S}{2\pi\hbar} - \tfrac{1}{2}.\]

The result is a non-negative real number. CoarseAIR’s default Final States Assigment Mthd = Histogram rounds to the nearest integer and assigns the trajectory to the matching level index in the pre-computed level table (produced in Level Generation (Numerov method)). The alternative methods (Gaussian binning, QSS) retain the float \(v'\) in the output so that post-processing can apply a different weighting scheme without rerunning the trajectories.

Consistency check. For a trajectory that starts from level \((v_i, J_i)\) and does not experience a reactive rearrangement, numerical energy conservation of the classical dynamics guarantees \(S_\text{final} \approx S_\text{initial}\) up to integrator error. Inspecting the distribution of \(v' \bmod 1\) in a large ensemble is the standard diagnostic for “zero-point leakage”: a histogram uniformly spread across \([0, 1)\) indicates trajectories slipping below their zero-point energy and is the signature of the known QCT artefact discussed in Bibliography (Bonnet–Rayez, 1997).

Code pointers. The above procedure is implemented as a small chain of elemental / pure routines on the diatomic-potential object — see PES Formats:

  • DiatomicPotential%TurningPoint → sub-step 3d (Newton–Raphson bracketing).

  • DiatomicPotential%ActionIntegral (or ActionIntegralGamma for the complex-\(\Gamma\) variant used for quasi-bound lifetimes) → sub-step 3e.

  • DiatomicPotential%FindMinimum / FindMaximum → \(R_\text{min}(J')\) / \(R_\text{max}(J')\) bracket for the turning-point search.

Each of these is called in the hot path of MolecularState%FindState (src/Collision/MolecularState/), so making them fast is important; they are all pure elemental and auto-vectorise over the batch of concurrently-finalising trajectories.

Step 4 — Classify the process

Given the initial and final state indices, the process type follows:

Process

Definition

Elastic

Same initial and final pair, same \((v, J)\) up to binning tolerance.

Inelastic

Same pair (no atom exchange), but different \((v, J)\).

Exchange

Different pair — the identity of the bonded atoms changed.

Dissociation

No bound pair survives (3-atom: A + B + C; 4-atom: breakdown to atoms or atom + diatom subsets).

Exchange-dissociation

4-atom only: one of the two product diatoms has dissociated while the other reformed as an exchanged pair (e.g. AB + CD → AC + B + D).

The Processes_Type hierarchy under src/Processes/ holds the cross-section and rate-coefficient accumulator for each process category; 3-atom and 4-atom systems use distinct subclasses (Nb3Atoms_Processes_Class, Nb4Atoms_Processes_Class) because the outcome space differs.

Indistinguishable-atom systems

When some atoms are chemically identical (e.g. H3, N3, O3, H4, N4, O4), the arrangement code alone is ambiguous: was this an exchange or just a re-labelling of the same diatom? CoarseAIR handles this in two ways:

  1. Indistinguishable mode — the cross-sections for exchange channels are summed together, matching the physical observable. This is the default for symmetric systems.

  2. Labelled mode — atoms are artificially tagged (e.g. NaNbNcNd is the labelled counterpart of N4, and O18O18O16 labels ozone by isotope). The code then tracks each exchange pathway separately. Labelled runs are used to audit the indistinguishable-mode averaging and to compute isotope-specific rates.

Both modes use the same classification algorithm; the difference lives in how the output is aggregated during post-processing.

Common pitfalls

  • Long-lived complexes. On deep-well PES surfaces (C-containing systems, quasi-bound O3 ensembles) a trajectory can spend tens of picoseconds in a four-atom complex before separating. If tmax is too short, the classification fires on the complex itself and labels the outcome as a mis-attributed “elastic” or “quasi-bound” event. Inspect the distribution of final times before trusting complex-rich systems.

  • Centrifugal barrier close to \(E_{\text{int}}\). The quasi-bound / dissociated boundary is a thin one for near-dissociation levels; small numerical noise in \(E_{\text{int}}\) can flip the classification. The CheckMaxAndMin and CheckTurningPoints validators flag borderline cases.

  • Zero-point leakage. Classical trajectories do not respect the \(v = 0\) zero-point energy; low-energy vibrational populations are under-represented. This is a well-known QCT artefact; see the cross-references in Bibliography.

Derivations and further detail

Why Langer’s \((J + \tfrac{1}{2})^{2}\). Direct quantisation of the 3-D central-field Hamiltonian gives the centrifugal term \(\hbar^{2} \ell(\ell + 1)/(2\mu R^{2})\) with \(\ell\) integer. The semi-classical (WKB) treatment introduces a near-origin singularity that Langer (1937) showed is corrected by replacing \(\ell(\ell + 1) \to (\ell + \tfrac{1}{2})^{2}\). This makes the WKB turning-point analysis at \(R \to 0\) quantitatively correct without touching the long-range behaviour. CoarseAIR uses the Langer correction consistently throughout level generation, final-state quantisation, and the centrifugal-barrier classifier.

Effective-potential derivation. Separating the 3-D radial Schrödinger equation into radial and angular parts, the radial equation for \(u(R) = R\,\psi(R)\) reads

\[-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2\mu}\frac{d^{2} u}{dR^{2}} \;+\; \left[ V_{\text{diat}}(R) \;+\; \frac{\hbar^{2}\,(J + \tfrac{1}{2})^{2}}{2\mu R^{2}} \right] u \;=\; E\,u,\]

which is one-dimensional with the effective potential \(V_{\text{eff}}(R; J) = V_{\text{diat}}(R) + V_{\text{c}}(R; J)\) defined in the main text above. For sufficiently large \(J\), the centrifugal barrier \(V_{\text{c}}(R; J)\) adds a maximum at some \(R_{\max}(J) > R_{\min}(J)\); states with \(V_\infty < E < V_{\max}(J)\) are classically trapped behind this barrier — the quasi-bound regime.

Heapsort for arrangement classification. Finding the tightest pair among \(\binom{N}{2}\) inter-atomic distances is an \(O(N \log N)\) operation by heapsort, implemented in src/Tools/heapsort.F90. For 3-atom systems (\(\binom{3}{2} = 3\) distances) the sort is trivial; for 4-atom systems (\(\binom{4}{2} = 6\) distances) it is still cheap. The same routine is used whenever the code needs to order quantities by value (e.g. sorting levels by internal energy during preprocessing).

WKB action integral — quantitative. At a bound eigenvalue \(E_{vJ}\) the Bohr–Sommerfeld condition

\[\oint p(R)\,dR \;=\; 2 \int_{R_\text{in}}^{R_\text{out}} \sqrt{2\mu\,[E_{vJ} - V_{\text{eff}}(R; J)]}\,dR \;=\; 2\pi\hbar\,\left(v + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\]

agrees with the exact radial Schrödinger eigenvalues to leading order in \(\hbar\). The correction terms scale as \(\hbar^{2}\) and are typically <1 % of the level spacing for heavy-atom diatoms — negligible for CoarseAIR’s purposes. The integral is evaluated by Gauss–Legendre quadrature (Nb of Quadrature Pts for Action Integral in CoarseAIR.inp, default 50 points). For the level generator’s use see Level Generation (Numerov method); for the final-state quantisation see the main text above.

Gaussian-binning alternative. The histogram binning CoarseAIR uses rounds the WKB action to the nearest integer. Bonnet and Rayez (1997) proposed Gaussian binning: weight each trajectory’s contribution to the final level \(v'\) by a Gaussian of width \(\sigma\) centred on the actual \(v\)-value, with \(\sigma \sim 0.1\) typically. This penalises trajectories whose final action lies far from any quantised level — a proxy for suppressing ZPE-violating outcomes. Not yet implemented in the base CoarseAIR; tracked on /developer/roadmap.

What the classifier does for 4-atom systems. The 3-atom decision tree generalises by partitioning the four atoms into the best diatom pair first (by smallest two of the six inter-atomic distances, subject to consistency: the two selected pairs must share no atom), then applying the effective-potential classifier to each candidate diatom independently. The 4-atom outcome space therefore includes: two bound diatoms (inelastic or exchange, depending on atom identities), one bound diatom + two free atoms (single dissociation), four free atoms (full atomisation), and various quasi-bound / predissociating mixes. The encoding in arr_f = 16 m + n captures one diatom at a time; 4-atom trajectory output carries \((J_k, v_k, J_l, v_l, \text{arr}_f)\) and the post-processor aggregates these into process channels (see Input File Reference for the process-index encoding).
