Sampling

Each trajectory needs six pieces of information before integration can start: the initial ro-vibrational state \((v, J)\) of the target diatom, the collision energy \(E\), the impact parameter \(b\), three orientation Euler angles, and the electronic-surface index iPES. This chapter describes how CoarseAIR samples each of them.

The relevant code lives under src/Collision/InitialStates/ — specifically StateInitDiatomAtom_Module.F90 (3-body) and StateInitDiatomDiatom_Module.F90 (4-body).

Ro-vibrational state \((v, J)\)

Internal states come from the pre-computed level database (dtb/Molecules/<species>/Bins_*/levels_cut.inp). CoarseAIR offers four selection strategies, chosen by the Bins/Nb of PESs and related input blocks and implemented in src/BinnedMolecules/:

  • State-to-State (StS) — one bin per \((v, J)\) level. This gives full state-resolved output but is expensive at high temperature.

  • Vibrational Specific (VibSpec) — bins are labelled by \(v\) only; rotational sub-levels within each \(v\) are distributed by their Boltzmann weight. Standard for vibrational-ladder studies.

  • Energy-based — bins group levels within internal-energy windows. Used for coarse-grained models (group-specific rates).

  • Hybrid / FromFile — user-supplied partitioning, read directly from a file listing levels per bin.

Within a selected bin, the specific starting level is drawn with probability proportional to its degeneracy-weighted Boltzmann factor.

Collision energy \(E\)

CoarseAIR provides four translational-energy-model options, selected via Relative Translational Energy Model = in the input file (see Input File Reference for the exact syntax of each):

Keyword

Energy distribution

FixedRelE

Delta-function \(\delta(E - E_{\text{rel}})\): every trajectory starts at the same specified energy.

Boltzmann

Maxwell–Boltzmann collision-rate-weighted density \(\hat{E}\,e^{-\hat{E}}\) at temperature \(T\) (see below).

Gaussian

Gaussian \(\mathcal{N}(\mu_E, \sigma_E^2)\) with user-specified mean and standard deviation.

FixedTotEn

Delta-function on the total energy \(E_{\text{tot}} = E_{\text{rel}} + E_{\text{int}}\). Useful for holding \(E_{\text{tot}}\) fixed while the ro-vibrational partition varies.

Each option feeds different trajectories to the same downstream dynamics; the choice is entirely one of what statistical ensemble is being sampled.

FixedRelE (delta-function sampling). Every trajectory starts at the same \(E_{\text{rel}}\) — no variance from the energy distribution. The resulting cross-section estimator is \(\hat\sigma_J(E_{\text{rel}})\), and by running on a grid of energies one builds up the full excitation function \(\sigma_J(E)\). Thermal averaging can then be done offline via numerical quadrature. This is the mode of choice when the user wants transparent control of what energies are being probed.

Boltzmann (Maxwell–Boltzmann thermal sampling). Energies are drawn from the translational Maxwellian at temperature \(T\). Starting from

\[k(T) \;=\; 4\pi \!\int_0^\infty \sigma(w)\, f(w)\, w^3\, dw, \qquad f(w) \;=\; \left(\frac{\mu}{2\pi k_B T}\right)^{3/2} \exp\!\left(-\frac{\mu w^{2}}{2 k_B T}\right),\]

the change of variables to reduced energy \(\hat{E} = E/k_B T\) gives the collision-rate-weighted density

\[p(\hat{E}) \;=\; \hat{E}\, e^{-\hat{E}}, \qquad F(\hat{E}) \;=\; 1 \;-\; e^{-\hat{E}}\,(\hat{E} + 1).\]

CoarseAIR draws \(U \sim \mathcal{U}[0,1]\), inverts the CDF by Newton–Raphson to obtain \(\hat{E}\), and sets \(E = \hat{E}\, k_B T\). The thermal rate then collapses to the arithmetic mean

\[k(T) \;\approx\; \sqrt{\frac{8 k_B T}{\mu\pi}}\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p = 1}^{N} \sigma(\hat{E}_p).\]

Gaussian sampling. Energies are drawn from \(\mathcal{N}(\mu_E, \sigma_E^2)\) with user-specified mean and standard deviation. The resulting rate is the numerical Gaussian average

\[\bar\sigma \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \sigma(E)\, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_E} \exp\!\left[ -\frac{(E - \mu_E)^{2}}{2\sigma_E^{2}} \right] dE,\]

estimated in practice by a sample mean over the drawn energies. Unphysical negative energy draws (always possible for a Gaussian) are resampled. The mode is most useful to reproduce narrow non-thermal distributions from upstream simulation data (laser excitation, shock-compressed gas) where neither a Maxwellian nor a single fixed energy is a good model.

FixedTotEn. Energies are drawn such that the total energy (translational + internal) is fixed at \(E_{\text{tot}}\). Given the sampled initial ro-vibrational state with internal energy \(E_{\text{int}}(v, J)\), CoarseAIR sets \(E_{\text{rel}} = E_{\text{tot}} - E_{\text{int}}(v, J)\) for that trajectory. This keeps the total energy constant as the \((v, J)\) partition varies — a useful probe for vibrational-threshold studies and for comparing state-specific reactivity at matched collision energies.

All four sampling code paths live in src/Random/ and the initial-state modules under src/Collision/InitialStates/; the branching on This%TtraModel is in src/Input_Class.F90.

Impact parameter \(b\)

By standard kinematics, the differential cross-section in \(b\) carries weight \(2\pi b\,db\), so uniform sampling in \(b^2\) over \([0, b_{\max}^2]\) reproduces the correct weighting without further Jacobians. CoarseAIR’s input offers four impact-parameter models:

  • Normal — uniform in \(b^2\), single \(b_{\max}\). Simplest and default for most dissociation / inelastic studies.

  • Random — uniform \(b\), requires Jacobian reweighting during rate extraction. Mainly for diagnostic use.

  • Stratified — \(b\) is split into concentric annuli \([b_{i-1}, b_i]\) with pre-assigned trajectory counts \(N_i\). Reduces variance at small \(b\) where reaction is most likely.

  • FixedAngMom — \(b\) is chosen to reproduce a target orbital angular momentum, useful for reactive-scattering studies at specified \(\ell\).

The value of \(b_{\max}\) is system- and energy-dependent; a brief pre-run “scattering map” (see H3 System) establishes the smallest \(b\) at which no reactive/inelastic events occur, and that value sets \(b_{\max}\).

Relative-velocity decomposition

Given \(E\), \(b\), and the initial separation \(D_{\text{init}}\) along the \(x\) axis in the body-fixed frame, the relative velocity magnitude and components are

\[V_{e_R} \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{2 E}{\mu}}, \qquad V_{e_z} \;=\; -V_{e_R}\,\frac{b}{D_{\text{init}}}, \qquad V_{e_x} \;=\; -V_{e_R}\,\sqrt{1 - \frac{b^2}{D_{\text{init}}^2}}.\]

The minus signs encode the convention that the projectile approaches the target (which sits at the origin). \(V_{e_y} = 0\) is imposed by placing the collision plane to coincide with \(x\)–\(z\).

The projectile’s initial Cartesian position and velocity vectors are then split between target and projectile reduced-mass fractions:

\[\mathbf{R}_T \;=\; -\frac{m_P}{m_T + m_P}\, D_{\text{init}}\, \hat{\mathbf{x}}, \qquad \mathbf{R}_P \;=\; +\frac{m_T}{m_T + m_P}\, D_{\text{init}}\, \hat{\mathbf{x}}.\]

Orientation sampling (Euler angles)

The target diatom’s orientation in the body-fixed frame is randomised by three successive Euler rotations. Using the standard \(z\)-\(y\)- \(z\) convention \((\alpha, \beta, \gamma)\):

\[\begin{split}\begin{pmatrix} x'\\ y'\\ z' \end{pmatrix} \;=\; R_z(\gamma)\, R_y(\beta)\, R_z(\alpha) \begin{pmatrix} x\\ y\\ z \end{pmatrix}.\end{split}\]

With \(\alpha, \gamma\) uniform on \([0, 2\pi)\) and \(\cos\beta\) uniform on \([-1, 1]\) (to sample the sphere uniformly), the resulting orientation is isotropic. The angles are drawn independently per trajectory. For 4-atom systems the same scheme is applied to both colliding diatoms independently.

Electronic-surface index iPES

When multiple electronic surfaces are active (e.g. O3 with 9 surfaces, CNO with 3, CO2 with 5), CoarseAIR distributes trajectories across surfaces proportionally to their electronic degeneracies.

At initialisation, the routine SetPESIndx() (called from the collision class) assigns each trajectory an iPES drawn from the discrete distribution

\[P(\text{iPES} = s) \;=\; \frac{g_s}{\sum_{s'} g_{s'}},\]

where \(g_s\) is the degeneracy of surface \(s\) specified in the input file. The per-PES trajectory counter Traj%iTrajPES(1:NPESs) tracks how many trajectories have been assigned to each surface, so the final statistics (cross-sections, rates) are weighted consistently.

In adiabatic CoarseAIR, iPES(iTraj) never changes after this initial assignment — see PES Formats for the multi-surface machinery and the CoarseAIR+ documentation for surface-hopping extensions.

Derivations and further detail

Why uniform in \(b^2\). The differential reactive cross-section at impact parameter \(b\) is, by definition, \(2\pi b\,P(b)\,db\) where \(P(b)\) is the reaction probability. Monte Carlo estimation of \(\sigma = \int 2\pi b\,P(b)\,db\) has minimum variance when the sampling density matches \(2\pi b\), i.e. when \(b\) is drawn with PDF proportional to \(b\). Substituting \(u = b^2\), \(du = 2b\,db\), this becomes a uniform distribution on \(u \in [0, b_{\max}^2]\). Equivalently, to generate such a \(b\), draw \(U \sim \mathcal{U}[0,1]\) and set \(b = b_{\max}\sqrt{U}\). CoarseAIR’s Normal impact- parameter model does exactly this; the Random model by contrast samples \(b\) uniformly and requires an explicit Jacobian reweighting downstream.

Stratified sampling variance reduction. Splitting the domain \([0, b_{\max}^2]\) into \(K\) disjoint strata of fractional weight \(w_i\) and allocating \(N_i\) trajectories to each stratum gives the estimator \(\hat\sigma = \pi b_{\max}^2 \sum_i w_i\, \hat P_i\) with variance

\[\operatorname{Var}(\hat\sigma) \;=\; (\pi b_{\max}^{2})^{2}\, \sum_{i=1}^{K} \frac{w_i^{2}\,P_i(1 - P_i)}{N_i}.\]

This is strictly smaller than the unstratified variance whenever the \(P_i\) differ across strata — the usual case, since reaction probability drops steeply with \(b\). The optimal Neyman allocation \(N_i \propto w_i\sqrt{P_i(1-P_i)}\) is approximated in practice by allocating roughly twice as many trajectories to small-\(b\) strata as to large-\(b\) ones; the exact per-stratum counts are a user choice in CoarseAIR.inp.

Inverse-CDF sampling derivation. Starting from the 3-D Maxwellian speed density \(f(w)\,4\pi w^{2}\,dw\) with \(f(w) = (m/2\pi k_B T)^{3/2} \exp(-m w^{2}/2k_B T)\), the substitution \(E = \tfrac{1}{2}m w^{2}\) (so \(w\,dw = dE/m\)) gives the collision-rate weighted energy density

\[p(E)\,dE \;=\; \frac{1}{(k_B T)^{2}}\,E\,e^{-E/k_B T}\,dE \;=\; \hat{E}\,e^{-\hat{E}}\,d\hat{E},\]

where \(\hat{E} = E/k_B T\). Its cumulative distribution \(F(\hat{E}) = 1 - (1 + \hat{E})\,e^{-\hat{E}}\) does not admit a closed-form inverse, so CoarseAIR solves \(F(\hat{E}) = U\) with \(U\sim\mathcal{U}[0,1]\) by Newton–Raphson iteration: starting from the initial guess \(\hat{E}_0 = -\ln(1-U)\) (which is exact if \(p = e^{-\hat{E}}\) were used in place of the collision-weighted density), the update

\[\hat{E}_{n+1} \;=\; \hat{E}_{n} \;-\; \frac{F(\hat{E}_n) - U}{\hat{E}_n\,e^{-\hat{E}_n}}\]

converges quadratically in about 5 iterations to \(10^{-12}\) relative accuracy. The code lives in the RNG utilities under src/Random/.

Isotropy of the Euler sampling. Uniform \(\alpha, \gamma \in [0, 2\pi)\) with \(\cos\beta \sim \mathcal{U}[-1, 1]\) produces an SO(3)-invariant distribution of molecular orientations. A quick way to see this: the Haar measure on SO(3), expressed in Euler angles, is \(dg = \sin\beta\,d\alpha\,d\beta\, d\gamma / (8\pi^2)\). Changing variables to \(u = \cos\beta\) gives \(dg = du\,d\alpha\,d\gamma / (8\pi^2)\), so the product measure of uniform \(\alpha, \gamma\) and uniform \(u\) reproduces Haar — i.e. isotropic orientation. This matters for getting the correct unweighted average of reactive cross-sections over molecular orientation.

Proportional vs equal allocation. With Using Proportional Allocation? = yes, the total trajectory budget \(N_\text{tot}\) is distributed across levels proportionally to their Boltzmann weights \(g_i e^{-E_i/k_B T_\text{int}}\). This concentrates trajectories where the population is actually large — efficient for thermal rate coefficients but wasteful for state-to-state studies where every level needs tight error bars. With Using Proportional Allocation? = no and Nb of Trajectories per Level / Bin specified, every level receives the same trajectory count. Both modes produce statistically consistent thermal rates; the choice is a variance-reduction question, not a correctness one.
