Level Generation (Numerov method)

Before any trajectory can be run, CoarseAIR needs the ro-vibrational energy-level table of every diatomic molecule that may appear as a reactant or product. The table ships pre-computed for most supported species (see dtb/Molecules/<Species>/), but for new potentials or new PES variants the levels must be recomputed. This chapter describes the numerical machinery behind that computation.

The relevant code lives in src/Levels/LevelsGenerator_Module.F90 and is called by the executable coarseair-computelevels.x whenever Generating Energy Levels? = yes in the input.

What is being solved

For each diatomic molecule with internuclear distance \(R\) and reduced mass \(\mu\), CoarseAIR solves the radial Schrödinger equation for the bound ro-vibrational states of the effective potential

\[V_{\text{eff}}(R; J) \;=\; V_{\text{diat}}(R) \;+\; \frac{\left(J + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{2}}{2\mu R^{2}},\]

where \(V_{\text{diat}}(R)\) is the chosen diatomic potential (see PES Formats) and the centrifugal term uses the Langer correction \(J(J+1) \to (J + \tfrac{1}{2})^{2}\). The goal is to find every eigenpair \(\{E_{vJ}, \psi_{vJ}(R)\}\) such that

\[-\frac{1}{2\mu}\frac{d^2 \psi_{vJ}}{dR^2} \;+\; V_{\text{eff}}(R; J)\, \psi_{vJ} \;=\; E_{vJ}\, \psi_{vJ},\]

with \(\psi_{vJ}(R)\) satisfying the usual bound-state boundary conditions at \(R \to 0\) and \(R \to \infty\).

Numerov integration

CoarseAIR integrates the radial Schrödinger equation with the Numerov method — a three-point recurrence specialised to equations of the form \(\psi''(R) = f(R)\, \psi(R)\). Writing \(\psi_n = \psi(R_n)\) on a uniform grid of spacing \(h\), the update is

\[\left(1 - \tfrac{h^2}{12} f_{n+1}\right) \psi_{n+1} \;=\; 2\left(1 + \tfrac{5 h^2}{12} f_n\right) \psi_n \;-\; \left(1 - \tfrac{h^2}{12} f_{n-1}\right) \psi_{n-1},\]

with

\[f(R) \;=\; 2\mu\left[\, V_{\text{eff}}(R; J) - E \,\right].\]

The scheme is fourth-order accurate and has excellent stability properties for oscillatory bound-state wavefunctions. For the canonical reference see Sandvik (2016) “Numerical Solutions of the Schrödinger Equation” and, for the coupled-channel generalisation, Johnson’s J. Chem. Phys. 69, 4678 (1978) — both cited in Bibliography.

Eigenvalue search

For each \(J\) the bound-state eigenvalues \(E_{vJ}\) are found by a two-stage bracketing:

  1. Bisection (40 iterations by default) narrows the interval \([V_{\min}, V_\infty]\) — the potential well plus any centrifugal barrier — to a small bracket around each eigenvalue using sign changes in the logarithmic derivative of \(\psi\) (the number-of-nodes criterion).

  2. Newton–Raphson refinement (80 iterations) polishes each eigenvalue to high precision using a derivative-based update.

Both stages call the Numerov forward/backward integrators and a matching condition at the classical turning point. The logic is implemented in LevelsGenerator_Module.F90.

Semi-classical action integrals

In addition to \(E_{vJ}\), CoarseAIR computes the WKB action integral

\[S(E, J) \;=\; \oint p(R)\, dR \;=\; 2 \int_{R_{\text{in}}}^{R_{\text{out}}} \sqrt{2\mu\,[E - V_{\text{eff}}(R; J)]}\, dR,\]

between the inner and outer classical turning points. At each computed eigenvalue this should give \(S \approx 2\pi\hbar\, (v + \tfrac{1}{2})\); deviations are used as a quality check. The action integral is the same quantity used at run time to quantise the final vibrational state of each trajectory — see Arrangement Classification.

The action is evaluated by Gauss–Legendre quadrature; the number of quadrature points is set in the input file (Nb of Quadrature Pts for Action Integral =, typically 50).

Bound, quasi-bound, unbound

For each \(J\), three kinds of solutions can appear:

  • Bound — \(E_{vJ} < V_\infty\) (\(V_\infty\) = the asymptotic potential). The standard case.

  • Quasi-bound — \(V_\infty < E_{vJ} < V_{\max}(J)\) where \(V_{\max}(J)\) is the centrifugal-barrier maximum. These states are metastable — classically trapped behind the barrier but asymptotically unbound. CoarseAIR retains them in the level table with a finite lifetime \(\Gamma_{vJ}\) (column 4 of the output file) computed from the barrier transmission.

  • Unbound — \(E_{vJ} > V_{\max}(J)\). Discarded.

The quasi-bound set matters physically because a significant fraction of near-dissociation population at high temperature sits in these levels. CoarseAIR’s level generator treats them as genuine levels; downstream post-processing can choose to include or exclude them (see Arrangement Classification).

Turning-point and extremum search

The Numerov solver needs accurate turning points \(R_{\text{in}}, R_{\text{out}}\) and, for quasi-bound levels, the position and height of the centrifugal-barrier maximum. The diatomic potential object (DiatomicPotential_Type) exposes FindMinimum, FindMaximum, and TurningPoint methods that locate these by bracketed root-finding on \(V_{\text{eff}}(R; J)\).

The CoarseAIR input lets you prescribe the search window for the barrier maximum:

Lower Bound for Position of Centrifugal Barrier Max = 1.0d0
Upper Bound for Position of Centrifugal Barrier Max = 30.d0

Getting this wrong (barrier outside the search window) is a common cause of “missing” quasi-bound levels; widen the window if the level table looks truncated in \(J\).

Output level file

The per-molecule level table written by coarseair-computelevels.x lives at <rundir>/Test/<System>/<Molecule>/<filename> where <filename> comes from GenerateLevels.inp. Each row is one level with 11 columns:

#

Column

Meaning

1

\(v\)

Vibrational quantum number.

2

\(J\)

Rotational quantum number.

3

\(E\) (Eh)

Internal energy.

4

\(\Gamma\)

Lifetime parameter for quasi-bound levels.

5

\(R_{V_{\min}(J)}\) (a.u.)

Position of the effective-potential minimum.

6

\(R_{V_{\max}(J)}\) (a.u.)

Position of the centrifugal barrier.

7

\(V^{\min}(J)\) (Eh)

Well depth of the effective potential.

8

\(V^{\max}(J)\) (Eh)

Height of the centrifugal barrier.

9

\(\tau_v\) (a.u.)

Vibrational period.

10

\(R_{\text{in}}\) (\(a_0\))

Inner turning point.

11

\(R_{\text{out}}\) (\(a_0\))

Outer turning point.

The same level file is consumed later by the trajectory initial-state sampler (see Sampling) and by the final-state quantiser (see Arrangement Classification).

What this replaces in a QCT pipeline

Some older QCT codes compute ro-vibrational actions on the fly during each trajectory’s final-state analysis. CoarseAIR does all of that work once, up front, at machine-precision for every \((v, J)\) it will ever need. This choice is what enables:

  • Efficient histogram binning of final trajectory outcomes without having to re-solve the Schrödinger equation in the hot path.

  • Clean separation between PES work (level generation) and dynamics work (trajectories), so the two can be debugged and validated independently.

  • Fast switching between binning strategies in post-processing — once levels exist, regrouping into state-specific, vibration-specific, or coarse-grained bins is essentially free.
