Quasi-Classical Trajectories

CoarseAIR computes state-to-state cross-sections and rate coefficients by the Quasi-Classical Trajectory (QCT) method. The name captures the central compromise: the nuclear dynamics are integrated classically on a single Born–Oppenheimer potential energy surface (PES), while the initial and final internal states are quantised semi-classically. In the regime it targets — thermal and super-thermal collisions between light atoms on modestly reactive surfaces — QCT delivers fully state-resolved cross-sections at a small fraction of the cost of a proper quantum scattering calculation, and with well-understood failure modes that can be audited a posteriori.

This chapter lays out the theory underlying every piece of the CoarseAIR runtime, in enough detail that a reader can connect the equations to the specific routines in src/Collision/. Subsequent theory chapters cover the algorithms that implement the ideas here (sampling, integrator, PES evaluation, state identification, rate extraction).

Historical context and code lineage

The modern QCT method was established by Karplus, Porter and Sharma for H + H2 in the mid-1960s and has been the workhorse method for reactive scattering ever since. CoarseAIR’s direct numerical ancestor is VVTC (Vectorized Variable-Stepsize Trajectory Code) by David W. Schwenke at NASA Ames Research Center: Bulirsch–Stoer integration of Hamilton’s equations in Jacobi coordinates, vectorised over a batch of trajectories, with impact-parameter sampling by stratified \(b^2\) discs and semi-classical binning of the final actions. CoarseAIR re-implements VVTC’s numerics in modern object-oriented Fortran 2008 and adds:

  • polymorphic PES / diatomic-potential / binning / processes / system objects that drop in from the input file;

  • a multi-surface infrastructure (\(N_\text{PES}\) up to 9 for O3) with per-trajectory surface indexing that provides the scaffolding used by the CoarseAIR+ fewest-switches surface- hopping extension;

  • machine-learned potential formats (feed-forward neural networks, permutation-invariant NN, Gaussian processes, Bayesian NN ensembles) on top of the traditional analytic forms.

None of the above changes the physics: a CoarseAIR calculation on, say, the NASA N3 surface is expected to agree with a VVTC calculation on the same surface to within Monte Carlo statistical error. The regression test test/systems/N3/ in the repo exercises exactly this — see Regression Tests.

The Born–Oppenheimer surface and the classical limit

Every CoarseAIR trajectory is propagated on a single Born–Oppenheimer surface \(V(\mathbf{R})\), i.e. the electronic Schrödinger equation has been solved (offline, by whatever method produced the PES) at every nuclear configuration \(\mathbf{R}\) to give a single scalar potential. Inside the dynamics, electronic degrees of freedom do not appear — they have been “frozen out” into \(V(\mathbf{R})\).

The classical-limit (or WKB) approximation then treats the nuclei as classical point masses moving on that single surface. The total quantum-mechanical time-dependent Schrödinger equation for the nuclear wavefunction \(\Psi(\mathbf{R}, t)\),

\[i\hbar\,\frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} \;=\; \left[ -\sum_\alpha \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_\alpha} \nabla_\alpha^{2} \;+\; V(\mathbf{R}) \right]\Psi,\]

is replaced by Hamilton’s equations for a classical point-particle trajectory \(\mathbf{R}(t)\) with canonical momenta \(\mathbf{P}(t)\). The ro-vibrational states that QCT cannot represent explicitly are recovered by binning trajectory-level observables (final arrangement, final action, final angular momentum) onto pre-computed quantum levels — see Arrangement Classification.

Regime of validity

The classical limit is controlled by the de Broglie wavelength of each nucleus relative to the length scale \(\ell\) on which the PES features change,

\[\lambda_\alpha \;=\; \frac{h}{p_\alpha} \;=\; \frac{h}{\sqrt{2 m_\alpha E_\alpha}}, \qquad \text{QCT valid when } \lambda_\alpha \ll \ell.\]

For atomic or ro-vibrational collisions at \(T \gtrsim 1000\) K, typical nuclear de Broglie wavelengths for even the lightest atom (H, \(m \sim 1836\,m_e\)) are \(\lambda \sim 0.5\,a_0\), well below the bond-length scale \(\ell \sim 1\text{--}3\,a_0\) of the PES minima. For heavier atoms (N, O, C, Ar) the ratio is an order of magnitude more favourable.

What the classical approximation misses:

  • Tunnelling. Any reactive channel whose classical barrier lies above the collision energy is mis-represented as closed. For shallow barriers \(\Delta V \lesssim k_B T\) this is usually a minor correction; for high, thin barriers — notably H-atom transfer reactions in the cryogenic regime — it can be orders of magnitude.

  • Zero-point-energy (ZPE) leakage. Classical trajectories do not respect the \(v = 0\) zero-point level; vibrational energy can flow freely below the quantum-mechanical ground state. Gaussian-binning (Bonnet & Rayez) and adiabatic constraints are known corrections; the base CoarseAIR code uses histogram binning without ZPE correction (see Arrangement Classification).

  • Quantum interference. Different reactive pathways to the same final state are summed incoherently — phases are not tracked.

  • Resonances. Sharp features in the energy dependence of a reactive cross-section, produced by metastable intermediate complexes, are either washed out or misattributed.

  • Nonadiabatic transitions. A single-surface PES cannot describe electronic hopping; these are addressed in CoarseAIR+ by the fewest-switches surface-hopping algorithm.

CoarseAIR’s runtime diagnostics (energy conservation, sum rule, detailed balance — see Rate & Cross-Section Extraction) detect numerical failures but not these physical approximations; the user is responsible for judging whether the QCT regime applies to their chosen system and temperature.

Jacobi coordinates and reduced masses

For a 3-atom system with atoms A, B, C and masses \(m_A, m_B, m_C\), CoarseAIR uses the atom + diatom Jacobi decomposition. Let A and B be the two atoms of the initial target diatom and C the projectile. Define:

\[\mathbf{r} \;=\; \mathbf{R}_B - \mathbf{R}_A, \qquad \mathbf{R} \;=\; \mathbf{R}_C - \frac{m_A \mathbf{R}_A + m_B \mathbf{R}_B} {m_A + m_B},\]

so \(\mathbf{r}\) is the AB internuclear vector and \(\mathbf{R}\) is the displacement of C from the AB centre-of-mass. The centre-of-mass motion decouples and is removed. The associated reduced masses are

\[\mu_{AB} \;=\; \frac{m_A m_B}{m_A + m_B}, \qquad \mu_{AB,C} \;=\; \frac{(m_A + m_B)\,m_C}{m_A + m_B + m_C},\]

and the total kinetic energy in these coordinates reads

\[T \;=\; \frac{\|\dot{\mathbf{r}}\|^{2}}{2}\,\mu_{AB} \;+\; \frac{\|\dot{\mathbf{R}}\|^{2}}{2}\,\mu_{AB,C}.\]

The corresponding conjugate momenta are \(\mathbf{p} = \mu_{AB}\dot{\mathbf{r}}\) and \(\mathbf{P} = \mu_{AB,C}\dot{\mathbf{R}}\), giving six position and six momentum components — hence NEqtTot = 12 for a 3-atom system. CoarseAIR packages these components into the phase-space vector PaQ (momenta first, coordinates second).

For a 4-atom system A, B, C, D the analogous diatom + diatom Jacobi decomposition uses:

\[\mathbf{r}_1 \;=\; \mathbf{R}_B - \mathbf{R}_A, \qquad \mathbf{r}_2 \;=\; \mathbf{R}_D - \mathbf{R}_C, \qquad \mathbf{R}_{12} \;=\; \mathbf{R}_{CD}^{CM} - \mathbf{R}_{AB}^{CM},\]

with three reduced masses (\(\mu_{AB}\), \(\mu_{CD}\), \(\mu_{AB,CD}\)) and NEqtTot = 18 phase-space components. The transformation logic lives in src/Collision/Transformation_Class.F90; the mass factors \(\mu\) enter mMiMn inside each PES object during initialisation, so the integrator sees them as plain constants during the hot path.

Pair numbering convention

A key convention carried throughout the code and the output files:

3-atom systems have three atoms A, B, C with three atom pairs:

\[\text{pair 1} = (A, B), \qquad \text{pair 2} = (A, C), \qquad \text{pair 3} = (B, C).\]

4-atom systems have four atoms A, B, C, D and six atom pairs (see the Notation figure in the legacy User’s Manual):

\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{llll} 1 = (A, B), & 2 = (A, C), & 3 = (A, D), & \\ 4 = (B, C), & 5 = (B, D), & 6 = (C, D). & \end{array}\end{split}\]

The convention is used by the AtomsPair module (src/AtomsPairs/), by the final-state classifier when it reports arr_f, and — through the 16 m + n encoding — by the trajectory-output file (Post-processing).

Hamiltonian and canonical equations of motion

Starting from the Lagrangian \(L = T - V\) in the Jacobi coordinates above, the Legendre transform gives the Hamiltonian

\[H(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{Q}) \;=\; \sum_{k = 1}^{N_{\text{eq}}} \frac{p_k^{2}}{2\, m_k^{\text{eff}}} \;+\; V(\mathbf{Q}),\]

where \(\{m_k^{\text{eff}}\}\) are the Jacobi reduced masses (mMiMn on the PES object) and \(V(\mathbf{Q})\) is the PES value on the trajectory’s assigned electronic surface. Hamilton’s equations

\[\frac{dQ_k}{dt} \;=\; \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_k} \;=\; \frac{p_k}{m_k^{\text{eff}}}, \qquad \frac{dp_k}{dt} \;=\; -\frac{\partial H}{\partial Q_k} \;=\; -\frac{\partial V}{\partial Q_k}\]

are the system integrated by the Bulirsch–Stoer method in Trajectory Integrators. The right-hand side is assembled in HamiltonODESystem (src/Collision/ODE/ODE_System.F90), which delegates the PES gradient to the PES object via Collision%Compute_PES:

! simplified: the per-trajectory PES dispatch
call PESsContainer( iPES(iTraj) )%PES% &
     Compute( Q(:,iTraj), V(iTraj), dVdQ(:,iTraj), Rpi(:,iTraj) )

The Hamiltonian \(H\) is a conserved quantity of the exact flow; numerically it drifts by a small amount each step, and the trajectory-level \(\Delta H = H_{\text{final}} - H_{\text{initial}}\) is monitored as the primary integration-quality diagnostic (see Trajectory Integrators).

Semi-classical quantisation at the boundaries

QCT uses quantum-mechanical state assignments only at the trajectory endpoints — never during the propagation. At both the initial and final times, CoarseAIR applies a semi-classical correspondence:

Vibrational action. For a bound diatom with reduced mass \(\mu\) and internal energy \(E_\text{int}\), the Bohr–Sommerfeld quantisation condition is

\[\oint p(R)\,dR \;=\; 2 \int_{R_\text{in}}^{R_\text{out}} \sqrt{2\,\mu\,[E_\text{int} - V_{\text{eff}}(R;J)]}\, dR \;=\; 2\pi\hbar\,\left(v + \tfrac{1}{2}\right),\]

with \(R_\text{in}\), \(R_\text{out}\) the classical turning points. Inverting for \(v\) gives the vibrational quantum number used in the output. This is the same integral that the level generator evaluates at each eigenvalue of the radial Schrödinger equation — see Level Generation (Numerov method).

Rotational action. The Langer-corrected mapping between the classical squared angular momentum and the rotational quantum number is

\[\|\mathbf{L}\|^{2} \;=\; \left(J + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{2}\hbar^{2}, \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad J + \tfrac{1}{2} \;=\; \frac{\|\mathbf{L}\|}{\hbar}.\]

CoarseAIR computes \(\mathbf{L}\) from the final-state atomic positions and velocities in the product diatom’s CM frame, then solves for \(J\). Histogram binning rounds \(J\) (and \(v\)) to the nearest integer before they are written to the trajectory output; the unrounded float values are retained so the user can post-process with a different binning strategy (Gaussian, QSS) if desired.

Both quantum numbers are then mapped onto the pre-computed level table for the product diatom — i.e., onto a specific \((v, J)\) index (state-to-state mode) or group index (coarse-grained mode) — using the binning strategy specified in CoarseAIR.inp. See Sampling for the binning framework and Arrangement Classification for the full final-state pipeline.

Per-trajectory runtime pipeline

Putting the pieces together, the life of one trajectory is:

  1. Initial-state sampling — draw \((v_i, J_i)\), collision energy \(E\), impact parameter \(b\), Euler orientation angles, and PES index iPES from the distributions described in Sampling.

  2. Build PaQ in the body-fixed frame at initial separation \(D_\text{init}\) along \(x\).

  3. Propagate by Bulirsch–Stoer until tmax or product separation RpiMax (Trajectory Integrators).

  4. Final-state identification — sort inter-atomic distances, compute the effective potential, classify as bound / quasi-bound / dissociated, compute action integrals, assign \((v', J')\) and arrangement code (Arrangement Classification).

  5. Emit one row of trajectories.csv with \((i_\text{traj}, \text{iPES}, b_{\max}, b, J_i, v_i, \text{arr}_i, J_f, v_f, \text{arr}_f)\).

The cross-section and rate-coefficient estimators that consume this output are derived in Rate & Cross-Section Extraction.

What CoarseAIR does not do in the adiabatic pipeline

A short consolidated list for the record (all are design choices, not bugs):

  • No dynamical electronic transitions. Each trajectory stays on the surface iPES was initialised to. Multi-surface runs are therefore a degeneracy-weighted average over independent single-surface ensembles, not a coupled-channels simulation. Surface hopping is a CoarseAIR+ feature.

  • No Gaussian-binning ZPE correction in the base code. Final \(v, J\) are assigned by histogram binning to the nearest pre-computed level. Post-processing tools can apply Gaussian binning or QSS-style weighting if desired.

  • No quantum interference between reactive pathways.

  • No backward-propagation cross-check in production runs. A Backward Integrating? = yes option exists for diagnostics but is not the normal mode.

  • No relativistic or spin-orbit corrections beyond what is already baked into the PES. Different spin-orbit components of the same electronic manifold are separate PES instances in the input (e.g. the three CNO electronic surfaces).

These limits set the boundary of the QCT regime; whenever a user’s application approaches one of them, the run should be cross-checked against an independent quantum or semi-classical method, or extended via CoarseAIR+.
